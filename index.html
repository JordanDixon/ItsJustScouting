Twins Mavery and Adele were the first two girls to join Cub Scouts in Marietta, Ohio.

Although Mavery and Adele were the first girls to join Cub Scouts Pack 207 of Marrieta, Ohio, to them it was as simple as making them official participants of a club they had been around for all their lives.

Boy Scouts of America has made the decision to officially allow girls to join the Cub Scout program in a new recruitment effort called, "Scout Me In."

Cub Scouts is a family-based program for elementary school age children. Each grade level has a rank and is associated with an animal or symbol for fourth and fifth graders. The Ranks meet by dens during the meetings, which are split by gender, coming together for activities and events.

EVERY FIRST AND THIRD Thursday of the month Mavery and Adele run into the American Legion that is filled to the brim with blue and khaki-clad Cub Scouts.
They are warmly greeted by a smiling Tony Durm, the Committee Chairman for the Pack, Troop, and Venture Crew. “My favorite thing is hiking with Tony,” said Mavery, “He tells us so much information.”

Every meeting begins with the pledge and scout oath. They break off into dens for activities. “It was kinda awkward, but it wasn’t that bad because a lot of my friends from school were there,” said Adele. The girls are currently Webelos, which is the den for the fourth and fifth graders.

Cub Scout Rankings

Cub Scouts is divided into rankings. These rankings are determined by elementary grade level. Mavery and Adele are Webelos, the last ranking before they are allowed to join Boy Scouts next year.

Bobcat

The first rank earned by every boy in Cub Scouting. No matter age or grade a youth joins Cub Scouting, the youth must earn his Bobcat badge before the youth can be awarded any other rank.

Tiger

A child who is in the first grade (or is 7 years old).

Wolf

The rank designed for a second-grade or 8-year-old Cub Scout.

Bear

The rank designed for a Cub Scout in the third grade (or 9 years old).

Webelo

(Pronounced WEE-buh-los.)
A Cub Scout who has completed the third grade or is age 10 but has not yet completed fifth grade or reached age 11 1/2, and is a member of a Webelos den.

MAVERY AND Adele were the first two girls to attend Seven Ranges Scout Reservation on the Webelo Resident Camp Program, which is for boys and girls who are transitioning from Cub Scouts to Scouts BSA. Seven Ranges has been operated under the Buckeye Council since 1987 and each summer over 5,000 campers gather in Kensington, Ohio.
Older Scouts earn work towards merit badges while at the Webelo Resident Camp the girls and other Webelos earn their colors and work towards their arrow of light, which is the only badge a cub scout can carry over to Scouts BSA.

The Webelo Colors are optional yellow, red, and green emblem onto which the Webelos Activity Badges and Arrow of Light Adventure Pins may be pinned. It is worn on the right sleeve immediately below and touching the U.S. flag. Mavery and Adele last summer earn their Craftsman badge and the Aquanaut badge.

HENRY IS not new to Camp Kootaga. “I’ve been to that camp so many times, it’s kinda like a second home to me,” he said. Henry, alongside other Scouts from his troop, volunteered one weekend; they woke up at 5 a.m. to cook breakfast for the entire camp.

GSUSA released a statement, and while not actually mention Scouts of BSA, they wrote, “We believe strongly in the importance of the all-girl, girl-led, and girl-friendly environment that Girl Scouts provides, which creates a free space for girls to learn and thrive. The benefit of the single-gender environment has been well-documented by educators, scholars, other girl- and youth-serving organizations.” The new transition for Boy Scouts of America means a new name. They plan on dropping the word boy from the title, a move to welcome girls, and go by Scouts BSA. The name change sparked concern from GSUSA. So much that they filed a federal trademark lawsuit in U.S. District Court for the Southern District of New York claiming that Scouts BSA will “marginalize” and damage GSUSA. Boy Scouts has two programs, one is Scouts BSA, which is for boys and girls 11-17. The other is the new Cub Scout campaign called “Scout Me In,” which promotes family scouting and involvement. According to NPR, “It’s the generic use of “Scouts,” without a gender marker, that the Girl Scouts object to.” The argument over the generic use of Scouts has long been an argument, dating back to the creation of Girl Scouts. “Khaki uniforms and adoption of military drills” was found threatening by Boy Scout leaders during that time. As they felt other youth organizations like the Camp Fire Girls were more gender appropriate,” Leslie Paris writes in her book Children’s Nature: The Rise of the American Summer Camp, “James West, BSA leader from 1911 to 1943, rallied particularly against the Girl Scouts’ use of the term ‘scout,’ fearing that if the term became feminized, it would become unsuitable for boys’ adventure.”
