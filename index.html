Twins Maevry and Adele McCombs were the first two girls in Marietta, Ohio to join Cub Scouts.

To Maevry and Adele, the decision to become the first girls to join Pack 207 was simple. To them, it was a matter of becoming official participants of a club they had been around for all of their lives.

The 9-year-old twins started coming to family campouts when they were four years old. They followed their older brother Henry, tagging along on hikes and other scouting activities.

Their mother Jamie remembers their early experiences with the scouts. “They would let the girls do the crafts with them. They’d let them come along to stuff. But still, when they sat down to do their memorization stuff, the girls were like, ‘Well, why can’t we need that?’ So, they realize that they weren’t part of it and they didn’t like that it was only for boys.”

Cub Scout events are now packed with girls and boys of all ages. It’s normal to see younger siblings tagging along and participating in Cub Scout events or campouts.

The McCombs family is a busy family of five; splitting their time between two scouting organizations was not practical. “Everyone wants their kids to grow, learn and be happy. We thought of Girls Scouts, but it wasn’t a right fit, and we were already involved in Boy Scouts because of Henry. As soon as we heard that girls could join, it wasn’t even a question,” said Jamie.

To the girls, the choice was easy. “I don’t think there is that much of a difference between the two,” said Maevry, “I just like Boy Scouts better.”

Cub Scout Rankings

Maevry and Adele are currently Webelos. Cub Scouts, each elementary grade level has a rank and is associated with an animal or symbol. The ranks always split into dens during meetings. Now that girls have joined, the dens are split by gender and come together for activities and events.

Bobcat

The first rank earned by every boy in Cub Scouting. No matter age or grade a youth joins Cub Scouting, the youth must earn his Bobcat badge before the youth can be awarded any other rank.

Tiger

A child who is in the first grade (or is 7 years old).

Wolf

The rank designed for a second-grade or 8-year-old Cub Scout.

Bear

The rank designed for a Cub Scout in the third grade (or 9 years old).

Webelo

(Pronounced WEE-buh-los.)
A Cub Scout who has completed the third grade or is age 10 but has not yet completed fifth grade or reached age 11 1/2, and is a member of a Webelos den.

History

The overall scouting movement started over 108 years ago, in 1910; the program was to engage in values set by the Boy Scouts of America. “Scouting is a values-based program with its code of conduct. The Scout Oath and Law help instill the values of good conduct, respect for others, and honesty. Scouts learn skills that will last a lifetime, including basic outdoor skills, first aid, citizenship skills, leadership skills, and how to get along with others.”



Two years later Girl Scouts, GSUSA, began in 1912 with the vision and direction of Juliette Gordon Low. Low wanted an organization that gave girls courage, confidence and built character so that girls would prepare for the world. According to the GSUSA website, “Juliette, affectionately known as “Daisy” by her family and close friends, gathered 18 girls in her hometown of Savannah, Georgia. So she could share what she had learned abroad about a new outdoor and educational program for youth, and with this, the Girl Scout Movement was born. Along with Juliette, these first Girl Scouts blazed trails and redefined what was possible for themselves and girls everywhere.”



Recent years membership has been declining in youth scouting organizations, which some claim is the push for change in Boy Scouts of America. However, the organization has been making more progressive strides in the past recent years. In 2015 BSA lifted the ban on openly gay scout leaders and had open membership for transgender youth, accepting the gender on application form instead of the birth certificate.



The Lawsuit


GSUSA released a statement, and while not actually mention Scouts of BSA, they wrote, “We believe strongly in the importance of the all-girl, girl-led, and girl-friendly environment that Girl Scouts provides, which creates a free space for girls to learn and thrive. The benefit of the single-gender environment has been well-documented by educators, scholars, other girl- and youth-serving organizations.”

The new transition for Boy Scouts of America means a new name. They plan on dropping the word boy from the title, a move to welcome girls, and go by Scouts BSA. The name change sparked concern from GSUSA. So much that they filed a federal trademark lawsuit in U.S. District Court for the Southern District of New York claiming that Scouts BSA will “marginalize” and damage GSUSA. Boy Scouts has two programs, one is Scouts BSA, which is for boys and girls 11-17. The other is the new Cub Scout campaign called “Scout Me In,” which promotes family scouting and involvement. According to NPR, “It’s the generic use of “Scouts,” without a gender marker, that the Girl Scouts object to.”

The argument over the generic use of Scouts has long been an argument, dating back to the creation of Girl Scouts. “Khaki uniforms and adoption of military drills” was found threatening by Boy Scout leaders during that time. As they felt other youth organizations like the Camp Fire Girls were more gender appropriate,” Leslie Paris writes in her book Children’s Nature: The Rise of the American Summer Camp, “James West, BSA leader from 1911 to 1943, rallied particularly against the Girl Scouts’ use of the term ‘scout,’ fearing that if the term became feminized, it would become unsuitable for boys’ adventure.”

MAVERY AND Adele were the first two girls to attend Seven Ranges Scout Reservation on the Webelo Resident Camp Program, which is for boys and girls who are transitioning from Cub Scouts to Scouts BSA. Seven Ranges has been operated under the Buckeye Council since 1987 and each summer over 5,000 campers gather in Kensington, Ohio.
Older Scouts earn work towards merit badges while at the Webelo Resident Camp the girls and other Webelos earn their colors and work towards their arrow of light, which is the only badge a cub scout can carry over to Scouts BSA.

The Webelo Colors are optional yellow, red, and green emblem onto which the Webelos Activity Badges and Arrow of Light Adventure Pins may be pinned. It is worn on the right sleeve immediately below and touching the U.S. flag. Mavery and Adele last summer earn their Craftsman badge and the Aquanaut badge.
